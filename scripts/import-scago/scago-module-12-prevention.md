### Slide 7 — Prevention of Sickle Cell Disease (Introduction)
**Type:** `text-sequence`
**LMS block:** `content_list` (or `rich_text` — 6 sequential paragraphs)

**Title:** Prevention of Sickle Cell Disease

**Content (6 items):**

1. Sickle cell disease (SCD) was initially endemic to sub-tropical and tropical regions. Carrier (trait) status conferred a level of protection from the lethal effects of malaria.

2. Migration of people from regions endemic to SCD has made SCD a global disease. High morbidity associated with SCD has placed a heavy burden on health care systems worldwide.

3. Research has shown while many people from ethnic groups at risk for SCD (people of Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, African, Caribbean, South Asian and South East Asian descent), may be familiar with the term SCD or know someone with SCD or sickle cell trait, they often do not have a good understanding of the hereditary pattern of SCD.

4. The distinctive sickling nature of red blood cells and associated illnesses of SCD were first described more than 110 years ago, yet little progress has been made to slow the global spread of SCD.

5. While the social, political and economic aspects of SCD are very important, this module will focus on the role health care providers can play to help minimize the global health burden of sickle cell disease.

6. Screening should be offered to all prepubescent or pubescent children, despite ethnicity as anyone can be carrier of SCD.

### Slide 13 — Pre-Marital Counselling
**Type:** `scrollable`
**LMS block:** `rich_text`

**Title:** Pre-Marital Counselling

- Patients who have SCD or known carriers of SCD can be counselled or reminded of the inheritance pattern of SCD and disease presentation.
- Some countries with a high prevalence of SCD have compulsory premarital screening programs for SCD. The primary aim of these programs is to prevent a marriage of people who have SCD or sickle cell trait.
- Canada has no premarital screening programs for SCD. The topic of pre-marital counselling is sensitive and not without controversy.

### Slide 14 — Premarital Counselling in Saudi Arabia
**Type:** `scrolling-media`
**LMS block:** `rich_text` + `image_gallery`

**Title:** Premarital Counselling in Saudi Arabia

**Content:**
- **Why is premarital counselling required in Saudi Arabia?**
- In 2004, premarital genetic screening was mandated in Saudi Arabia based on the high prevalence of SCD and Beta-Thalassemia, impact of these diseases on the quality of life of patients, and the financial impact on the healthcare system.
- **Image:** `https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1709478989/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/prevalence_aiys8w.png` *(SCD prevalence map)*
- According to Memish & Saeedi (2011), the higher frequency of consanguineous marriages, older ages of maternal and paternal family, and large family size may contribute to the high prevalence of SCD and Beta-Thalassemia in Saudi Arabia.
- **Image:** `https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1709463754/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/graph_marriage_u1g694.png` *(consanguinity data graph)*

### Slide 15 — Genetic Counselling: Expandable List
**Type:** `expandable-list`
**LMS block:** `content_list` (expandable items) or `rich_text`

**Title:** Genetic Counselling: Understanding Your Genes

3 expandable sections:

**Section 1 — Who Should See a Genetic Counsellor?**
- Patients who have SCD or family members who are carriers of SCD may choose to seek out genetic counselling. The genetic counsellor can offer screening for multiple family members or potential spouse/life partners.

**Section 2 — What Do Genetic Counsellors Do?**
- Genetic counsellors work closely with patients to assess their personal and family medical history, analyze genetic testing results, and help individuals understand their genetic risks and the implications of genetic information.

**Section 3 — Why See a Genetic Counsellor?**
- Genetic counsellors can offer pre-conception testing for parents at high risk for a baby with SCD, including IVF.

### Slide 17 — Summary
**Type:** `list`
**LMS block:** `content_list`

**Title:** Summary:

- Many people are unaware of their sickle cell status and misinformed of the hereditary pattern of SCD.
- Screening for SCD should begin pre or early adolescence or ideally prior to 10 weeks' gestation.
- Parents who screen positive for sickle cell trait or have SCD can be referred to a genetic counsellor during pregnancy.
- All newborn babies in Ontario are offered Newborn Screening (NBS) for rare genetic and metabolic disorders, including SCD. Parents will only be notified if their baby has SCD; however, parents have to request the results for sickle cell trait status.
- Compulsory premarital screening programs for sickle cell disease are common in many countries that have a higher rate of SCD — no such programs exist in Canada. Awareness, knowledge and education about SCD is valuable for everyone.
