### Slide 3 — rich_text
**Type:** rich_text
**Title:** Cognitive Bias

"Repeated pattern of thinking that may help people make quicker decisions but can lead to inaccurate or unreasonable conclusions." Hammond et. al., 2021

A way to think of cognitive bias is as an **algorithm that the mind uses** to help us to sort ideas and thoughts more quickly.

---

### Slide 4 — content_list (expandable)
**Type:** content_list
**Title:** Implicit Bias

**Items:**
- **Alteration:** "Associations or attitudes that reflexively alter our perceptions, and thereby affect behavior, interactions, and decision-making." Marcelin et al, 2019
- **Automation:** In medicine, unconscious bias will lead a clinician to automatically opt for a specific viewpoint, which impacts the way care is delivered.

![Implicit bias image](https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1700492696/applications/606cf6e5245c23de93c55d1d/documents/Module_8_-_ABR_and_SCD.pptx_2_noxyd7.png)

**Type:** image_gallery
**click_for_more_info:** true

**Prompt:** Explore the image

**Waypoints / Captions:**
1. Implicit/Unconscious Bias in Clinical Medicine = SYSTEM 1 THINKING (Adapted from JACC Basic Transl Sci. 2021 Jan; 6(1): 78–85.)
2. Let's take a moment now to review Dual Process Theory, which tries to explain how thought can arise in two different ways...
3. or as a result of two different processes that are either unconscious (or System 1 thinking) or consciously controlled (System 2 thinking).
4. In a nutshell, System 1 thinking processes manifest almost instantly and are often rooted in subconscious mental processes...
5. that are based on our personal histories and experiences. Implicit bias arises from System 1 processes.
6. Biased thoughts will result from automatic, intuitive and often irrational thought processes that influence our decision-making.
7. They are hardwired, subconscious, or gained by repeated experience. We resort to them because they are fast and they are familiar.
8. In medicine, there are many forms of cognitive bias, including implicit bias, and we revert to these thinking processes when...
9. we need to make quick decisions, when we are under stress, or when we are in familiar and seemingly repeated situations.
10. In medicine, cognitive bias will create mental shortcuts that we apply subconsciously to simplify our decision-making.
11. The problem is that these biases can often lead to incorrect conclusions, and it is well-documented in the medical literature that these shortcuts can lead to medical errors.

3. Learn about systems of inequality.
4. Step back – make space for those on the other side of the coin.
5. Change is needed at the interpersonal level, but also at the systemic and institutional levels.
6. Allyship **does not equal** enhancement of personal power.
