### Slide 7 — Course Introduction
**Type:** `text-sequence`
**LMS block:** `content_list` (or `rich_text`)

**Title:** Partnering with Primary Care Providers

**Content (6 sequential items):**

1. Primary care providers play an integral role in the overall health and well being of individuals they care for, and in specific, individuals with SCD.

2. Due to advances in medical treatments, individuals with SCD are living longer lives and there is limited knowledge on the care needs amongst healthcare providers across sectors (acute, specialist, and primary care) on the comprehensive care needs for these patients.

3. The role of PCPs is vast and includes, but is not limited to: counselling, health promotion, education, disease prevention, health maintenance, and diagnosis. Some PCPs may be less familiar with the complexities of SCD.

4. By partnering with their patients, who are the experts of their condition, and other health professionals, such as haematologists, ophthalmologists, and pain specialists, PCPs can help to align care with the established standards, overall improving the quality of life and outcomes for their patient(s).

5. Another important point to raise is the lack of specialists in SCD that exists. Depending on location, not all individuals with SCD have an option of going to a specialist.

6. Leveraging partnerships with PCPs will further help to drive down the costs to the healthcare system. Episodic, acute ED visits would be reduced, patients getting lost to follow up would decline, etc., due to collaborative care.

### Slide 18 — Summary
**Type:** `list`
**LMS block:** `content_list`

**Title:** Summary:

- Although there are greater than 60% of PCPs who do not feel comfortable providing care to individuals with SCD, partnering with haematologists, sickle cell clinics, and the patient/family will help to reduce the discomfort.
- Opioid use disorder (OUD) is uncommon for individuals with SCD; reduce screening for OUD and increase screening for disease modifying/chelation therapy, as appropriate.
- Patients and families/caregivers can sense the discomfort and lack of confidence of their PCP. Ask your patient questions — they are the expert and they can help guide you through the new and unfamiliar aspects of care.
