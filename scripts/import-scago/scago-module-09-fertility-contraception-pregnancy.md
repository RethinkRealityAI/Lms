### Slide 9 — Factors Impacting Fertility in Individuals with SCD
**type:** content_list
**title:** Factors Impacting Fertility in Individuals with SCD
**items:**
- **Hydroxyurea:** Exposure to hydroxyurea may lead to diminished ovarian reserve, while there are significant benefits to hydroxyurea that warrant individualized discussion regarding its use.
- **Ovarian Reserve:** It has been suggested that women with SCD have a lower and accelerated decline in ovarian reserve.
- **Uterine Fibroids:** African individuals have a high prevalence of uterine fibroids which may impact fertility.
- **Males with SCD:** There are a lack of data around fertility for women, while men with SCD have been shown to have infertility due to: • Hypogonadism • Sperm abnormalities • Erectile dysfunction (ED) due to priapism

**type:** rich_text
**title:** Reducing the Number of SCD Births
**content:** Screen the partner for sickle cell trait with a CBC and hemoglobin electrophoresis. ### Slide 20 — Contraception and Pre Conception In Sickle Cell Disease
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Contraception and Pre Conception In Sickle Cell Disease
**content:**
- To aid in informed decision making for individuals with SCD, we will briefly review contraception and pre-conception.
- Although there are limited data on the reproductive health for individuals with SCD, there are clear suggestions of: • higher rates of unintended pregnancies • with lower than average use of contraception
- Among other factors, decisions about contraception should be encouraged.
- Contraception is the prevention of pregnancy and examples include: • barrier methods • intrauterine devices • progesterone-only hormonal contraception (progestin only pill, Depot progestin or progestin-secreting intrauterine system) which has been shown to potentially lower vaso-occlusive episodes (VOE)
- Oral contraceptive pill, patch, or ring – contraindicated in women with a history of venous thromboembolism
- For those trying to prevent pregnancy, we must facilitate access to safe and effective contraception.
- For those who would like to conceive a child, they must be provided with appropriate pre-conception screening and genetic counseling for individuals with SCD and their partners.

### Slide 22 — Summary
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Summary:
**content:**
- People with SCD can experience infertility due to the same causes at the general population but may be more likely to have diminished ovarian reserve
- Several options exist for achieving pregnancy and people at risk of having a child with SCD should be counselled about options including IVF with PGT-M
- Fertility preservation is an important consideration in people facing a potential stem cell transplant

### Slide 8 — Optimizing Organ Function (Continued)
**type:** content_list
**title:** Optimizing Organ Function and Reducing Drug Exposures Before Pregnancy (Continued)
**items:**
- **Multidisciplinary Team:** Pregnant women with SCD must be cared for by a high risk obstetrical team knowledgeable in SCD and include a multidisciplinary team: • Maternal Fetal Medicine/Obstetrics • Haematology • Anaesthesia • Neonatology. Referrals are ideally sent prior to pregnancy, but if pregnant as soon as pregnancy is confirmed
- **Medication Management:** • Discontinue iron chelation (deferasirox, etc.) Note: for critical iron overload, deferoxamine can be used safely in the second and third trimesters, and while breastfeeding • Discontinue hydroxyurea (research supports consideration of hydroxyurea on an individualized basis) • Discontinue NSAIDS, ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers • Add prenatal vitamins without iron for those with iron overload • Add Folic Acid 5mg, to help replace folate lost during hemolysis • Ensure individual has drug insurance for costly medications such as LMWH
- **Other Considerations:** • Phenotype and genotype of red cell antigens including RhD • Blood group and antibody screen • Dental hygiene • Ensure stability of health conditions to optimize chance of healthy live birth

### Slide 11 — Maternal/Fetal Risks: Preterm Preeclampsia
**type:** content_list
**title:** Maternal/Fetal Risks in Pregnancy: Preterm Preeclampsia
**items:**
- **Preterm Preeclampsia:** • 12% of patients with SCD develop pre-eclampsia
- **Maternal Manifestations:** • Hypertension • Proteinuria • Other end organ dysfunction
- **Fetal Manifestations:** • intrauterine growth restriction • preterm birth • oligohydramnios • stillbirth • placental abruption
- **Preeclampsia Prevention:** • ASA 150-162 mg every evening • Initiate before 16 weeks' gestation to decrease risk of preeclampsia

---

### Slide 12 — Maternal/Fetal Risks: Cardiac Iron Overload
**type:** content_list
**title:** Maternal/Fetal Risks in Pregnancy: Cardiac Iron Overload
**items:**
- **Prevalence:** Happens less frequently than in beta thalassemia: 2.5%
- **Ferritin and LIC:** Small case series suggest the following as indicative of iron overload: • Ferritin > 4600 ug/L • LIC > 22 mg/g (mean 44 mg/g)
- **Risks:** Earlier and more frequent transfusion increases risk
- **Management:** Cardiac MRI T2* in the 2nd trimester and consideration of deferoxamine if iron overload

---

### Slide 13 — Pulmonary Complications
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Maternal/Fetal Risks in Pregnancy: Pulmonary Complications
**content:**
- Manifestations: Restrictive lung disease, airway hyper-responsiveness, pulmonary hypertension, and acute chest syndrome
- Small airway closure
- FEV1 < 65 may predict risk of fetal death: • OR 7.81, 95% CI 1.59–38.38, p = 0.01 (Hayfron-Benjamin, 2021)
- Management: Pulse oximetry at each visit. Consider home oxygen support in setting of hypoxia

### Slide 16 — Pain Management in Pregnancy
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Pain Management in Pregnancy
**content:**
The following can be administered chronically for pain during pregnancy: • Acetaminophen • Morphine • Hydromorphone • Chronic use of NSAIDs is discouraged at any point in pregnancy, as it is associated with fetal renal compromise and oligohydramnios. It is contraindicated after 28 weeks' gestation given risk of premature closure of the ductus arteriosus

**Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS)**

• Results from the abrupt discontinuation of opioids at birth after a fetus has become physically dependent • Opioid induced physical dependence is a transient phenomenon and treated supportively. It is important to emphasize that optimal treatment of maternal pain (often achieved only with use of opioids) is paramount to avoid ACS and severe maternal morbidity, which can in turn increase fetal risk of adverse events • Opioids with slow clearance rates (buprenorphine) have less severe withdrawal vs intermediate (methadone) or fast (morphine) rates • In a study by Volkow (2016), 131 patients with SCD were using opioids and 4% had NAS

### Slide 19 — What is Hyperhemolysis?
**type:** rich_text
**title:** What is Hyperhemolysis?
**content:**
- The destruction of both transfused and autologous RBCs
- Can be immediate or delayed
- Accompanied by reticulocytopenia, worsening anemia
- Can induce multiorgan failure and death most likely due to damage to the underlying vasculature by released free Hb and heme
- Further transfusion increases risk of recurrence!
- Medications used in pregnancy for treatment include: • Rituxan • Eculizumab • Erythropoietin • Toculizumab • Prednisone

---

### Slide 20 — Identifying Risk for Delayed Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions (DHTR)
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Identifying Risk for Delayed Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions (DHTR) in Pregnancy
**content:**
• Transfusion during pro-inflammatory events increases the risk of alloimmunization • Individuals with low number of previous transfusions or previous delayed hemolytic transfusion reactions are at high risk for DHTR - consider extended matching for Rh, K, Kidd, Duffy, and MNS • Jk, Ss, Fy Abs associated with a high risk of subsequent alloimmunization - extended matched advised • Autoantibodies, natural antibodies (anti-M, anti-Lewis) associated with low risk of subsequent immunization - CEK matched

When total hemoglobin falls below pre-transfusion levels, suggests an accompanying fall in patient's own cells, indicating hyperhemolysis. Delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction in sickle cell indicated by rapid fall in HgbA cells.

### Slide 21 — Fetal Monitoring
**type:** content_list
**title:** Fetal Monitoring
**items:**
- **Fetal Exposure to Potentially Teratogenic Medications:** If exposure to teratogenic medication has taken place in the 1st trimester, consider the following ultrasounds at: • Early anatomy ultrasound (~15 weeks' gestation) • Level II anatomy ultrasound (~18-20 weeks' gestation)
- **Placental Assessment:** Given a number of adverse pregnancy outcomes are placentally mediated, consider the following testing: • Placental biochemistry e.g. (PAPP-A in first trimester, PlGF around 23 weeks' gestation), if available • Ultrasound evaluation of the placenta including uterine artery Doppler. In the late second and third trimesters, serial ultrasounds for fetal growth and well being should be performed: • Estimated fetal weight • Assessment of fetal umbilical artery Doppler • Amniotic fluid volume • Biophysical profile

### Slide 22 — Summary
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Summary:
**content:**
- Pregnancy is associated with the potential for severe maternal, fetal and pregnancy adverse events
- Prepregnancy counselling is essential to reduce those risks by educating about planning a pregnancy, discontinuing potentially teratogenic medications, optimizing organ function
- Multidisciplinary management once pregnancy is confirmed includes a Haematologist/MFM (or Obstetrician with expertise in SCD in pregnancy), Anaesthesia, and Paediatrics near birth
- Regular maternal and frequent fetal monitoring during pregnancy is necessary to reduce risks
- Transfusion during pregnancy is individualized, requires consultation with a Haematologist, and balances risks vs. benefits

### Slide 3 — Considerations for Labour and Delivery
**type:** content_list
**title:** Considerations for Labour and Delivery
**items:**
- **Monitoring:** • Fetal - continuous electronic fetal monitoring • Maternal - vitals including SpO2 (> 95%), maintain fluid balance, analgesia, warmth
- **Medications:** • Early neuraxial anesthesia to minimize pain and anxiety
- **Consultations:** • OB/MFM • Haematology • Anaesthesia • Neonatology
- **Best Option for Delivery:** • Delivery should take place in an expert centre, with availability of relevant maternal and fetal monitoring • Aiming for spontaneous vaginal delivery • Cesarian section, reserved for obstetrical indication
- **The Little Things:** • Maternal hydration should be emphasized, either via the oral or intravenous route • Maintenance of maternal warmth and comfort • Adequate pain relief • Supplemental oxygen, as required • Spinal anaesthesia is recommended to reduce post partum VOE

### Slide 5 — Optimal Intrapartum Care
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Optimal Intrapartum Care — ASH (2020) Guidelines
**content:**
- Good analgesia to manage pain
- Epidural analgesia is safe and effective for pregnant women with SCD.
- Avoid dehydration to reduce risk of sickling crisis
- Regular monitoring of oxygen saturation
- Avoid protracted/prolonged labour.
- Continuous electronic fetal monitoring due to increased risk of unexplained stillbirth and adverse perinatal outcomes for pregnant women with SCD.

---

### Slide 4 — What is Obstetric Racism?
**type:** content_list
**title:** What is Obstetric Racism?
**items:**
- **Definition:** When Black women are trying to access maternal health services and they are subjected to beliefs and practices that lend to the reproducing Black body being harmed and manifests as forms of violence and abuse.
- **History:** Historically, Black women became victims of gynaecological surgeries without anaesthesia that were experimental, due to the racist belief that Black people have a high pain tolerance.
- **Perpetuation:** This has set the precedence for how Black childbearing women are expected to be treated within the healthcare system and by healthcare providers.

---

### Slide 5 — Why Are We Talking about Racism in Obstetrical Care?
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Why Are We Talking about Racism in Obstetrical Care?
**content:**
According to Early et al. (2023) there were 3 categories for pregnant people with SCD that demonstrated statically significant differences from the control group: • Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy • Labor and delivery complications • Hematologic and immunologic complications

The maternal mortality rate in deliveries among people with SCD was 26 times greater than in the non-Black control group and more than 10 times greater than among Black pregnant people without SCD

### Slide 6 — Quote on Racism as Determinant of Health
**type:** speech_bubble

**author:** Karen Fleming, Clinical Nurse Specialist

### Slide 7 — Manifestations of Obstetric Racism (Table)
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Manifestations of Obstetric Racism
**content:**
| Feeling | Manifestation |
|---|---|
| Invisibility | Standing in proximity to healthcare providers, their presence were unacknowledged |
| Ignored/ Neglected | Prejudice about Black women's capacities to endure pain led to healthcare providers refusing to provide pain relief during labour |
| Dehumanized | Women reported lack of compassion and loss of humanness in care |
| Disempowered | Decisions made for them and not with them. Lack of control and ability to influence their own care |
| Stigma | Participants felt an impending sense of failure and feelings of guilt from the internalization of stigma experienced from healthcare providers |

### Slide 9 — Reduction of Peripartum Racial and Ethnic Disparities
**type:** rich_text
**title:** Reduction of Peripartum Racial and Ethnic Disparities
**content:**
- In an effort to reduce peripartum racial and ethnic disparities, a workgroup made up of representatives from Obstetrics, Maternal Fetal Medicine, Midwives, Maternal/Child, Family Physicians, and a School of Public Health was developed
- This workgroup focused on the development of an evidence-based patient safety bundle.
- The proposed bundle was to be feasible and actionable, and have the potential to improve the quality of maternal health care, while reducing racial and ethnic disparities in maternal morbidity and mortality.
- Their work culminated in the development of a bundle that can be used by those who find them of benefit.
