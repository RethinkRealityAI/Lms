**Content items:**
1. Positive mental health or emotional functioning is an essential contributor to the health and well-being of individuals with SCD. With the benefit of research and observations from clinical settings, we understand better than ever the close relationship between mind, mood, cognitive functioning and physical health.
   - **Video:** https://media.edapp.com/video/upload/v1711357835/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/opening_vid_roqsqy.mp4

2. Good mental health and well-being in individuals with SCD may be associated with: Happiness or life satisfaction levels • Spirituality • Positive outlook • Hope • Thinking and problem-solving • Coping • Adjustment • Degree of social support • Sense of agency or control over life circumstances • Freedom from unnecessary suffering
   - **Image:** https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1711358842/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/wellness_ruoq3z.png
   - **Credit:** www.blogs.psychcentral.com, 2024
3. Patients may experience or report the above as: Having a clear mind and the ability to process information and set goals • Ability to pursue meaningful work and to function on the job • Having language to communicate wants and needs with ease • Experiencing restorative sleep • Access to timely and effective healthcare
   - **Image:** https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1711444846/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/good_MH_xbndpy.png

### Slide 9 — Text Sequence: Mental Health Across the Lifespan
**Type:** `text-sequence`
**LMS block:** `content_list` (animated steps)

**Title:** Mental Health Challenges Across the Lifespan

**Items:**
1. There is a growing body of research focusing on mental health in individuals with SCD across the lifespan. So let's look at some of the general trends by age group, starting with Children and Adolescents.
2. Increased risk of mood, anxiety, attention, and behaviour regulation challenges compared to peers without SCD.
3. Stress and depression may be worsened by pain severity and frequency of pain episodes.
4. High risk of overt and 'silent' stroke in the early childhood period may have linkages to behavioural, learning and social emotional sequelae for many youth.
5. Isolation and disruption in peer relationships is a contributor to mood, anxiety, and social skill difficulties.
6. Development of independent and self-help skills or 'developmental tasks of adolescence' may be slowed or delayed by interruption of home, school, family, and social routines due to a challenging illness course, including the unpredictable nature of pain episodes.
7. Body image disturbance and self-esteem challenges may be exacerbated by differences in physical maturation, physical appearance, endurance and stamina levels, and attainment of related milestones compared to peers without SCD.

---

### Slide 10 — Expandable List: Mental Health Challenges in Adults
**Type:** `expandable-list`
**LMS block:** `content_list` (expandable)

**Title:** Mental Health Challenges in Adults with SCD

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Mood and Anxiety Difficulties** | Adults with SCD are at significant risk for mental health difficulties, especially mood (depression) and anxiety disorders. |
| **Prevalence** | Prevalence rates of greater than 40% for depression and approximately 25% for anxiety have been reported in research samples of adult sickle cell patients. |
| **Disability** | Interference of SCD symptoms on daily functioning increases risk of low mood (depression), anxiety, and social isolation due to difficulties engaging with school and work. |
| **Stroke** | Stroke and risk of stroke recurrence continues to be of concern during the adult years and is correlated with decreased physical, cognitive and emotional functioning. |
| **Chronic Pain** | Chronic pain may co-occur with acute pain episodes at this life stage, and severity of disease-related complications tends to be greater with increasing age. |
| **Pain Management Challenges** | Adults with SCD continue to report significant undertreatment of pain in emergency or inpatient settings. Importantly, problematic addiction, substance misuse or 'drug seeking' patterns have not been substantiated in patients with SCD, who benefit from well-developed acute and chronic pain management support to reduce suffering. |

### Slide 18 — Summary
**Type:** `list`
**LMS block:** `content_list`

**Title:** Summary

- Mental health is something that everyone possesses
- As individuals living with chronic illness, youth and adults with sickle cell disease are at increased risk for mental health concerns, especially mood and anxiety challenges
- Living with complications of sickle cell disease can negatively affect mental health and wellbeing, as can encounters with healthcare practitioners and systems that are not well-informed about this condition, the need for timely treatment, and maintains barriers to support and needed resources
- There are a number of indicators of positive mental health and well-being that individuals with SCD and their caregivers may identify, including hope, connection to spirituality and social support and positive coping
- Conducting timely mental health screening and providing appropriate mental and physical treatment interventions can significantly improve mental health and well-being outcomes for individuals with SCD

1. As healthcare workers, we operate in a society that is characterized by power imbalances within systems, including healthcare, education, housing, employment, immigration, and justice.

   - **Image:** https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1711620041/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/ABR_xf1y93.png

### Slide 5 — Scrolling: Strengths-Based Practice / Resilience-Building
**Type:** `scrolling-media`
**LMS block:** `rich_text` + `image_gallery`

**Title:** Strengths-Based Practice / Resilience-Building

**Content:**
1. An approach that focuses on strengths as opposed to deficits:
   - People are resourceful and resilient
   - Strengths include knowledges, skills, coping strategies, relationships, values, hopes, and dreams
   - People develop strengths and resilience in the face of adversity
   - Individuals and families can identify their own strengths. As healthcare workers, we can also support people with sickle cell disease and identify strengths.
   - **Image:** https://media.edapp.com/image/upload/v1711620654/applications/620474488a388be8f86470f7/documents/collab_gdl0wg.png
2. Examples of strengths in people with sickle cell disease, their families, and their communities:
   - Faith and Spirituality
   - Existing coping strategies
   - Worldview and outlook
   - Sense of purpose and values
   - Support networks and community roots
   - Past experiences and knowledge of solving problems
3. Resource: "Cosplaying to Cope" shows how one person with Sickle Cell Disease draws upon their unique skills and talents to manage their condition and live a fulfilling life. https://www.rbcd.ca/blog/my-sickle-cell-disease-warrior-blood-moon-cosplaying-to-cope
