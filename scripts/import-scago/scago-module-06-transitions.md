### Slide 8 — Defining Transitions in Sickle Cell Disease
**LMS Blocks (stacked):** `rich_text` + `image_gallery`
**Slide title:** Defining Transitions in Sickle Cell Disease

**Text content (scrolling-media — multiple blocks):**
- Transition in healthcare involves addressing medical, psychosocial, and educational needs as care shifts from child and family-centered to adult-oriented.
- This process should begin around age 12, to start emphasizing self-management skills, vocational planning, and adolescent health education.
- The exact count of sickle cell disease (SCD) patients in Ontario, Canada, remains unknown. A 2023 study estimated that about 1 in 4200 people in Ontario had SCD from 2007/08 to 2016/17. The median age among this population was **24 years old**.
- *(Caption note: Click on image to zoom in.)*
- This figure presents the age-specific mortality rates subdivided into four time periods (1979–1988, 1989–1998, 1999–2003, and 2004–2009). Note the decline in mortality over the years for ages < 14 years old and then starts to fluctuate and increase as age increases.
- Transition can be particularly challenging for AYAs with SCD. Some challenges include: managing chronic illness, navigating social transitions, and the shift in care approach.

### Slide 10 — Current State of Healthcare Transition for AYAs with SCD
**LMS Blocks (stacked):** `rich_text` + `image_gallery`
**Slide title:** Current State of Healthcare Transition for AYAs with SCD

**Text content:**
- AYAs with SCD between 18–30 years old, compared to other age groups, have been shown to be more vulnerable during healthcare transition due to:
  - Unpredictable symptoms
  - Frequent pain episodes
  - High healthcare utilization
  - More rehospitalizations related to pain management
  - Higher morbidity/mortality rates
- A study of data between 1999–2009 revealed higher mortality rates among AYAs aged 20–24 years, aligning with the transition from paediatric to adult care, with rates of 0.6/100,000 for 15–19-year-olds and 1.4/100,000 for 20–24-year-olds.
- *(Caption: Click on the image to zoom in. Photo credit: Brown Med-Peds, n.d.)*
- This figure presents the age-specific mortality rates subdivided into four time periods. Note the decline in mortality over the years for ages < 14 years old and then starts to fluctuate and increase as age increases.

### Slide 13 — Navigating Multiple Transitions
**LMS Blocks (stacked):** `rich_text` + `image_gallery`
**Slide title:** Navigating Multiple Transitions

**Text content (scrolling-media):**
- AYAs with SCD face multiple simultaneous life transitions alongside transitioning from paediatric to adult healthcare services.

**School:**
- SCD complications like pain, cognitive deficits, and frequent hospitalizations harm school attendance and performance.
- Transitioning from high school to college can be tough for AYAs with SCD without proper planning. A study found 60% of 12–20-year-olds with SCD said it impacted their education, with 40% repeating grades, and those experiencing more hospitalizations reported it leading to lower grades.

**Content (text sequence — 4 steps):**

### Slide 21 — Summary
**LMS Block:** `content_list` — bullet_style: disc, enable_animations: true
**Slide title:** Summary

**Items:**
1. Transition in healthcare involves addressing medical, psychosocial, and educational needs.
2. AYAs with SCD face unique barriers during transition including stigma, racial bias, and limited access to adult SCD expertise.
3. The **6 Core Elements of Health Care Transition** provides a structured framework for successful transitions.

**Slide title:** True or False?

**Statement:** Hold private appointments solely with the parent/s of AYAs during their transition into adulthood.
- **Correct answer: False**

**Feedback correct:** That's correct!
**Feedback incorrect:** Not quite...
**Explanation:** This statement is **false**. **Include** youth in the conversation about their comprehensive care plan. Giving AYAs an opportunity to have private appointments (without parents/caregivers) allows them to build independence and self-advocacy skills. Parents/caregivers should remain involved but the focus should shift to patient-centred care.

### Slide 12 — Additional Barriers and Supports
**LMS Block:** `content_list` — display_mode: list, enable_animations: false
**Slide title:** Additional Barriers and Supports

**Content (text sequence — 3 steps):**
1. There are numerous barriers to transition including adolescent self-management skill set, previous negative experiences in the emergency department, neurocognitive deficits due to stroke, sociodemographic factors, and psychosocial/emotional status.
2. The stigma around accommodations at school and work can prevent AYAs from disclosing their condition and accessing the support they need.
3. Healthcare providers can support AYAs by helping them develop self-advocacy skills and connecting them to relevant resources and peer support networks.

### Slide 4 — Purpose and Aims of the Transition Navigator Role (Reveal/Accordion)
**LMS Block:** `content_list` — display_mode: accordion, accordion_icon: caret
**Slide title:** The Transition Navigator Role
**Prompt:** Select each item for more details

**Accordion items:**
1. **Purpose of the Role**
   To support adolescents and young adults (AYA) with red blood cell disorders in preparing for and throughout the transition from paediatric to adult health care.

2. **Aims of the Role**
   To improve rates of successful transfer from paediatric to adult care, reduce morbidity/mortality and promote self-management skills for AYA with red blood cell disorders.

### Slide 18 — Summary
**LMS Block:** `content_list` — bullet_style: disc, enable_animations: true
**Slide title:** Summary

**Items:**
1. Transition Navigators are a value-add to the transition of AYA patients.
2. Transitions should be started at age **12** and continued beyond the transition at 18 years of age.
3. Incorporate the use of standardized tools for transition readiness, such as the **Transition Readiness Assessment Questionnaire** (Brown & Sobota, 2016).
4. Transition involves more than just healthcare — it also includes other life transitions such as transition to university/college, work, etc.
